RM-4 zoning permits 1,200 SF/unit lot density on a 43,416 SF parcel — up to 36 units by-right. Existing 24 units sit 12 units below that cap, plus state ADU pathways for accessory units, providing long-term upside (subject to municipal review).

Clean Operating Record

Anaheim Quality Rental Housing Program inspections in 2015, 2021, 2022, and 2025 — all closed cleanly. No active liens, demolition orders, or chronic violations. Roofs replaced 2002–2003; consistent owner-managed maintenance posture.

Comp Read-Out

The strongest direct match is 1803 W Sumac Lane — a 14-unit, 1960-vintage property that traded September 2025 at $239,286/unit and a 4.78% cap rate. Sumac Lane is essentially adjacent to the subject and is the most active comp street in the submarket. 1768 W Juno Avenue (currently under contract through Marcus & Millichap's Leeson Group at $271,071/unit, 5.03% cap) provides the active competitive ceiling.

Closer in vintage, 3300 W Orange Avenue (1976, 9 units) traded January 2026 at $302,778/unit and 5.05% cap — the closest 1970s comp, demonstrating that mid-1970s product holds a $/unit premium over 1960s stock.

At $241,667/unit, the subject's list price sits just above the lowest Tier 1+2 sold price ($239,286 at 1803 Sumac) and well below the median (~$300,000/unit). $237/SF reflects subject's materially larger average unit size (1,019 SF vs. comp range 692–912 SF) — when units scale up, $/SF compresses but $/unit holds. Subject's 3.97% in-place cap sits below the comp band (4.78%–5.50%) only because the comparable assets trade closer to market rents. At full mark-to-market, every comp would re-price 100–200 bps higher; subject's 38% loss-to-lease justifies the in-place cap discount.

Pricing Rationale
Why $5.8M Reflects the Right Value

Subject is priced at $5,800,000 ($241,667/unit, $237/SF) — a positioning that delivers a defensible asking basis and meaningful value-add narrative for the OC private-capital buyer pool.

On a per-unit basis, the list price is below 6 of 7 verified Tier 1+2 sold comps in the submarket and 14.7% below the closest active peer (El Pueblo at 401 S Beach, also 1978 vintage). On a per-SF basis, $237 sits below the $345–$405/SF comp range — the explanation is unit size: subject's 1,019 SF average is materially larger than the comp set's 692–912 SF, and per-SF metrics compress predictably as units scale up.

On a cap-rate basis, the headline 3.97% in-place yield falls below the comp range (4.78%–5.50%) only because subject is uniquely under-rented. In-place average rent of $1,400/unit is below every comp's underlying rent profile — a 38% loss-to-lease versus validated $1,950–$2,400 market rents on Sumac Lane and Nutwood Street. At full mark-to-market, the property re-prices to a 6.37% cap on the same in-place SF and existing operations, exceeding the comp range ceiling without entitlements, capital improvements, or repositioning capex.

The trade range of $5,600,000 – $5,950,000 reflects a 50bp cap-rate spread around list. Conservative qualified buyers underwrite to a stabilized 6.0–6.5% yield to justify acquisition basis; more aggressive value-add operators with proven OC rental execution can stretch closer to the high end of the range.

Key Read-Outs

At a 5.25% stabilized exit cap rate (mid-band of the comp set), the refi-and-hold strategy delivers a 16.6% IRR with $154K of tax-deferred cash-out and continued $60K/year cash flow on the retained $3.0M equity stake. The sale strategy at the same cap delivers a 8.4% IRR but pulls all equity out.

Returns are highly sensitive to exit cap. A 25bp tightening to 5.0% adds approximately 11 percentage points to refi return; 50bp loosening to 5.75% materially erodes the case. Sponsors with execution confidence in lease-up should prefer refi to preserve optionality and tax efficiency.

Buyer profile fit: sophisticated OC value-add operators with in-house construction management capability, prior interior reno track record, and access to bridge debt. Mom-and-pop holders and pure 1031 buyers should expect lower returns on the same business plan due to higher execution risk and softer cost discipline.
